Barrick vs Newmont — do they do what they say?

A three-part, primary-sourced study of whether the two gold supermajors deliver on their own guidance — across capital spending and gold production — plus the 30-year capex backdrop. Every number is scripted from SEC filings and results releases; quotes are verbatim. Each deliverable was hardened through an adversarial review panel to all-lenses-excellent. A generalizability check on two more majors (Agnico, Kinross) tests whether the pattern is sector-wide — it isn't.
By JAOD & (mainly) Claude · attributable basis, each company's own · FY2015–2025 + 2026–2028 forward

The unified finding: they guide the favorable side of every operational metric

Across the three things a gold miner guides each year — capital spending, gold production, and unit cost (AISC) — Barrick and Newmont set the optimistic number on all three, and reality comes in worse on all three. They under-spend capex (which reads as discipline), under-deliver production, and over-run cost:

The cost over-run was relentless — AISC came in above the top of the guidance range in 5 of 7 years for Barrick and 6 of 7 for Newmont (2021–25), the overage widening to +$100/oz-plus at the 2022–23 inflation peak. Honest caveat: part of that is mechanical, not indiscipline — royalties scale with the realized gold price, which ran far above each year's guidance assumption, and both companies attribute a chunk of the overage to exactly that. But the direction is unambiguous: guidance was set on the low-cost side every year.

And they "hit guidance" by managing it: on capex and production they walk the opening number down mid-year and land within the revised guide (Newmont cut 5 of 7 capex revisions). Two fairness notes: the capex figures are full-basis (Barrick's was -1.5% ex its 2015–16 deliberate cuts); and on production Newmont is the tighter of the two (11/11 within its final guide vs Barrick's 9/11).

The forward signal connects the two: 2026 is a production trough for Newmont and roughly flat for Barrick (its reported dip to ~3.1 Moz is mostly the Hemlo/Tongon divestitures, not organic decline) — but only Barrick is funding a real step-up, guiding $4.0–4.5B of capex (its biggest in a decade, 62% growth-weighted, copper-led) to lift output to 3.40–3.75 Moz by 2028. Newmont guides flat capex (~$3.35B) into a sequencing trough and harvests cash.

Is it a sector rule? No — it tracks lifecycle (a 2×2)

Running the same guidance-vs-actual test (FY2019–2025) on two more majors — across both capex and production — refutes a universal pattern. The behaviour sorts by where each company sits in its build cycle:

The two largest, most mature supermajors (Barrick, Newmont), whose production base is shrinking, set ambitious budgets and output targets and come in light on both — "guide high, deliver light" is their reflex, not the sector's. The growth-phase Agnico does the inverse: it spends above guidance (+8%) to push Detour/Kittila/Odyssey and hits its production guidance on the nose (-0.5%, within 4/7 years) — a builder executing, not a manager sandbagging. Read a senior's "discipline" and a grower's "overrun" as the same coin seen from opposite ends of the build cycle.

The investor read. Take Barrick's big 2026 numbers with the company's own record in hand: it has under-produced its opening guidance ~3% a year and routinely revises down — so the $4.0–4.45B build and the 2028 output step-up are credible directions but likely to land at the lighter end, and the Reko Diq slice is already under security review. Weigh Newmont's "discipline" against the flip side: a post-2019 record of missing its own opening production targets, and a habit of cutting capex guidance mid-year (5 of 7 revisions). Neither company is dishonest — both simply guide high and manage down. The differentiator today is direction: Barrick is building (and tilting to copper); Newmont is harvesting.
