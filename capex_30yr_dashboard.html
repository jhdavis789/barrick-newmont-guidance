Real vs nominal — is capital spending actually rising?

The grey line is what the 8 majors spent in dollars of the day; the navy line restates it in constant 2025 dollars (US CPI). The gap between them is pure inflation. The 30-year arc the recent-years view misses: a 2011–12 supercycle peak (~$23B real in 2012), a brutal 2013–2016 discipline crash (trough ~$7.1B real in 2016, −69%), then a slow grind back up from 2017 that — even with gold near $4,300 — leaves 2025 ~38% below the 2012 real peak.

Two essential caveats on that headline: (1) The 2012 peak is part Barrick artifact. Barrick alone was 39% of 2012 capex, dominated by its Pascua-Lama project — suspended 2013, ~$8.5B written off, never produced an ounce. Excluding Barrick, the 2025 real shortfall vs 2012 narrows from ~38% to ~25% (the other 7 majors: ~$10.3B real in 2025 vs ~$13.7B in 2012) — so Pascua-Lama explains a chunk of the apparent underinvestment, but the 7 majors are still ~25% below their real 2012 peak. (Only in nominal dollars is ex-Barrick 2025 (~$10.3B) marginally above 2012 (~$9.8B) — i.e. before inflation.) (2) CPI understates mining cost inflation. Mining construction costs (steel, equipment, AUD/ZAR labour) ran several times the CPI rate through the 2004–12 supercycle (no single published index spans it cleanly — the direction is well-evidenced by AUD/ZAR strength, oil, steel and ENR-construction indices, the magnitude less so), so a mining-cost-deflated real-investment rise since 2004 is closer to ~1.5–2× than the 2.88× shown here, and the 2012 real peak is if anything overstated — which cuts the other way on the headline: the physical-capacity shortfall vs 2012 is actually smaller than the 38% the CPI line shows. (The AISC panel below is the industry's own cost gauge, but it begins only in 2013 — after that supercycle cost inflation had already run, which is why post-2013 real AISC is roughly flat.)

AISC $/oz (2013+) — the industry's own cost gauge

All-In Sustaining Cost per ounce. AISC is a WGC metric that only exists from mid-2013; before then the comparable figure is the (non-comparable) total cash cost, so the series starts in 2013. Pan American (silver-primary) is excluded — its AISC is silver-$/oz, not gold-comparable; the mean (red) is the 7 gold miners, up 1.4× ($1133→$1587/oz) since 2013. The honest read: that 1.4× nominal rise almost exactly matched CPI (1.38× over 2013–25), so in real terms sustaining cost per ounce has been roughly flat (1.01×) since 2013 — the faster-than-CPI mining-cost inflation was the 2004–2012 supercycle, which predates the AISC series. (2014 mean is 6 miners — Agnico 2014 AISC unavailable; all other years are 7.) Two per-company basis breaks when reading individual lines: Gold Fields 2019 is on the revised WGC standard ($897/oz; ~$970 old basis) and AngloGold shifts to managed-ops at 2017 — see their AISC notes below.
